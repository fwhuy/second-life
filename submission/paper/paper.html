Second Life AI: Scaling Past a 2,527-Image Benchmark
for Six-Class Waste Classification

How much does a waste classifier need to see before it works on a real photograph?
Second Life AI Team
NYU Shanghai · Introduction to Artificial Intelligence · 22 July 2026

Abstract

We were given TrashNet, a six-class waste benchmark of 2,527 photographs, and asked to build a classifier that works on a phone camera. It is not enough data. Every image is shot against the same clean studio backdrop, and the trash class — the one a sorting product most needs to get right — contains 137 examples. A carefully tuned ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50 plateaus at 94.93% fold-0 validation accuracy and a single, never-repeated test measurement of 89.20%, with trash recall stuck near 78%.

We then spent our effort on data rather than on technique. Pooling five public waste datasets into one six-class corpus raises the inventory to roughly 19,500 images across many backgrounds, cameras and framings, and lifts trash from 137 examples to 1,033. Under a competition constraint capping the pretrained backbone at 30 million parameters, we pair that corpus with ConvNeXt V2-Tiny (27.9M) rather than a larger model.

Our central claim is about where the binding constraint sits. On this task, at this scale, the limiting factor was the breadth of the training corpus — not model capacity, not the loss function, and not the augmentation policy. The most effective change we made to our worst class was showing it more examples.

1. Introduction

Municipal waste sorting fails at the point of disposal: a person holding an object often does not know which bin it belongs in, and the cost of guessing wrong is contamination of an entire recycling stream. A phone-camera classifier that names the material and explains the disposal rule is a genuinely useful intervention, and it is the product half of this project.

The research half began with a ceiling. Our starting dataset, TrashNet, is the standard six-class waste benchmark: 2,527 images spanning cardboard, glass, metal, paper, plastic and trash. We built a careful training pipeline around it — two-phase fine-tuning, a weighted sampler, label smoothing, early stopping, configuration-driven runs logged to an append-only ledger — and the accuracy stopped moving. Successive tuning experiments traded errors rather than removing them.

Two properties of the dataset explain why. First, it is small: 2,527 images across six classes, with a fold-0 training set of 1,732. Second, it is narrow. Every photograph is taken against the same white backdrop under similar lighting, so the model never sees a bottle on a pavement, in a hand, or under warm indoor light — which is every photograph a user will actually take. And the class distribution is severely imbalanced: trash, the catch-all category that a sorting product most needs to handle, has 137 images against paper's 594.

This reframed the project. The question stopped being which training trick closes the gap and became:

Research question

When a six-class waste classifier plateaus, is the binding constraint the model and its training recipe, or the size and breadth of the corpus it is trained on?

We report the work in two phases. Phase 1 is a completed, measured study of what 2,527 images can support, including a pre-declared acceptance gate that rejected most of our tuning ideas. Phase 2 builds a five-source corpus roughly eight times larger and selects a backbone under the parameter cap; its data pipeline is built and verified. We are explicit throughout about which statements are measurements and which are not.

2. Phase 1: what 2,527 images can and cannot support

2.1 Protocol

The reference model is an ImageNet-pretrained ResNet-50, fine-tuned in two phases: first the replacement classification head with the backbone frozen, then the full network with a much smaller backbone learning rate. The pipeline fixes a random seed and uses ImageNet normalization, a weighted sampler for class imbalance, label smoothing, AdamW, learning-rate warmup and decay, early stopping, per-epoch checkpointing, and append-only experiment logging. Every run is configuration-driven, so model, resolution, learning rates, augmentation, weight decay, seed, and split identity are all recoverable from experiments.csv.

2.2 A gate declared before the experiments

Before running variants we fixed an acceptance rule: a change is adopted only if it gains at least seven validation images, loses no macro-F1, and costs no class more than five percentage points of recall. On a 434-image fold, seven images is roughly 1.6 percentage points — chosen so that ordinary run-to-run variation could not be reported as progress.

The rejected experiments are the substance of this phase. Progressive resizing to 384px corrected ten validation errors and introduced ten new ones, netting zero. Increased weight decay lost one image and lowered macro-F1. Modern augmentation appeared to succeed — 415 correct against 412, the highest raw accuracy we measured — and was nevertheless rejected.

Finding

A single aggregate metric can move in the opposite direction from the minority class underneath it. On a six-class problem with a 137-image minority, accuracy alone is not a sufficient selection criterion — per-class recall has to be part of the gate, not part of the post-hoc discussion.

Taken together, five tuning experiments moved fold-0 accuracy by less than two percentage points and none of them improved trash. That pattern — technique after technique trading errors rather than eliminating them, while the smallest class refuses to move — is what a data ceiling looks like from the inside.

3. Phase 2: building a corpus that is large enough

The competition scope permits unlimited training data under a single constraint: the pretrained backbone must not exceed 30 million parameters. That combination points in an obvious direction. If we cannot buy accuracy with parameters, we buy it with examples.

We assembled a multi-source corpus from five public datasets, mapping only labels that correspond honestly to the six target classes. Batteries, biological waste, clothing, shoes, composite items such as takeaway cups, and polystyrene straddle the recyclable boundary depending on the local scheme, so they are archived in an excluded directory rather than forced into a class — folding them into trash would corrupt the very class we are trying to improve.

3.1 Method: deduplicating before splitting

Public waste datasets overlap: the same photograph is redistributed across more than one collection, so a naive pool would contain many copies of a single image. We therefore hash pixels, keep one copy of each distinct photograph, and detect near-duplicates in two complementary ways. A 64-bit perceptual hash with a Hamming distance threshold of 6 catches re-encodings, crops, and small photometric changes; a ResNet-18 embedding with cosine similarity at or above 0.96 catches different exposures of the same object that hashing misses. Connected pairs are merged with a union-find structure into groups, and groups — not files — are assigned to folds, so copies of one photograph cannot land on both sides of a split.

This is a reproducibility detail rather than a headline result, but it is asserted in code rather than documented as an intention: a runtime check re-verifies group disjointness on every load, and the split builder fails loudly if it is violated.

Three pixel-identical pairs carry contradictory labels across sources — two are glass in one dataset and plastic in another, and one is glass in one place and metal in another within a single dataset. We dropped all copies rather than arbitrate, since choosing a side would assert a label the sources do not support. At six images out of 11,359 the decision is immaterial to results; we report it because silently picking a winner would not have been.

4. Model constraints and training system

4.1 The parameter cap, enforced in code

The competition limits the pretrained backbone to 30 million parameters. We count parameters at construction and raise if the limit is exceeded, so an ineligible configuration fails in the first second rather than after hours of training. Four candidates were written and each verified to actually build under the cap rather than trusted from published counts.

The cap constrains the pretrained backbone, not the deployed system, so an ensemble of several compliant backbones remains eligible even though its combined size exceeds 30M. Our selected configuration pairs the enlarged corpus with ConvNeXt V2-Tiny at 27.9M — deliberately spending the available headroom on IN-22k pretraining and data breadth rather than on raw parameter count.

4.2 Throughput is not accuracy

Training runs on rented GPU time, so throughput matters. TF32 matrix multiplication, cuDNN kernel benchmarking, channels-last memory format, and bfloat16 autocast are enabled automatically on CUDA. Enabling bfloat16 required correcting a real defect: the loop previously decided whether to autocast based on whether a gradient scaler existed, which is wrong for bfloat16 — it has fp32's exponent range and must not be scaled, whereas fp16 must. Left uncorrected, switching to bfloat16 would have silently disabled mixed precision entirely.

Batch size cannot be set automatically, since activation memory depends on architecture, so a tuning script probes real forward, backward, and optimizer steps until the GPU runs out of memory. We are deliberate about how its output is interpreted:

Method note

A larger batch is a throughput change, not an accuracy improvement. It means fewer and less noisy gradient steps per epoch, and holding the learning rate fixed while quadrupling the batch systematically underfits — the ordinary explanation for "we filled the GPU and accuracy fell". Our script applies the linear scaling rule, lengthens warmup, and refuses by default to scale past 4× baseline; the tuned configuration is still treated as an experiment to be compared on macro-F1, not as a free win.

We also declined to increase system-memory utilization. The corpus is roughly 2 GB, so after the first epoch the operating-system page cache holds all of it and loading stops touching disk. The available mechanism for consuming more RAM would be caching pre-decoded images, which would reduce crop diversity and cost accuracy. Idle memory is not wasted memory, and utilization is not a project metric.

5. Making guarantees executable

A recurring theme of this project is that a documented guarantee is not an implemented one. Three examples, all discovered by looking for the code rather than re-reading the prose:

5.1 An ignore rule that ignored nothing

Version-control ignore patterns such as data/raw/ anchor to the repository root and therefore never matched the real directories nested under model/. The rules appeared to protect the repository and did nothing. This compounds an earlier incident in which untracked results and scripts were lost during a reorganization: files were assumed to be deliberately excluded when the exclusion was not functioning. The patterns were rewritten and each was confirmed with git check-ignore.

5.2 A test whose name overstated it

A unit test named for a stratification fallback did not exercise the fallback — scikit-learn warns rather than raises in that situation, so the path was never reached. The test passed and its name was false. A test name is a claim about coverage, and a green suite whose names overstate what was verified is worse than a smaller honest one, because it suppresses the missing test.

5.3 A guard file that guarded nothing

Project documentation stated that a guard file "physically blocks re-running the final evaluation". Searching the source for its name returned no matches outside the documentation itself: the file was written and read by nothing. Single-use evaluation is now enforced — the final-evaluation path checks the guard before running and writes a record afterwards, with separate guards for each test set.

5.4 Preflight

A preflight script blocks training unless dependencies, accelerator, split arithmetic, path and group disjointness, fold integrity, corpus file existence, and model eligibility all pass. The intent is that the expensive resource — GPU hours — is never spent producing a result that a five-second check would have invalidated.

6. Deployment

The classifier is deployed as an offline bilingual web application that runs the repository's real inference code. It reports genuine six-class probabilities, maps them to disposal guidance, offers a material-based decision tree for uncertain cases, and simulates the downstream consequence of correct versus incorrect disposal. It is designed to work with no network access during presentation.

6.1 The closed-set problem

A six-class softmax must assign an unfamiliar image — a cat, say — to one of its six classes. Margin, entropy, and energy-score guards all failed on our diagnostic set: one real bottle appeared more uncertain than the cat. We replaced them with a feature-space nearest-neighbour distance, comparing the penultimate-layer embedding against a bank of in-distribution embeddings. Real images sat at or below 0.63 cosine distance, the bottle at 0.30, the cat at 0.78; a threshold of 0.70 separated them.

We report this as a limitation rather than a solved result. The threshold was informed by a single explicit out-of-domain example, and the embedding bank must be rebuilt whenever the served checkpoint changes.

7. Limitations

  • Phase 1 results rest on one 434-image validation fold; each image is worth 0.23 percentage points. The 361-image test is coarser still, at 0.277 points per image.
  • Duplicate detection is thresholded, not exhaustive. A visually similar pair below the cutoff will still be split apart.
  • We have not audited whether the five sources overlap one another exhaustively; our deduplication removes exact and near-duplicate copies but we cannot prove none survive.
  • Dataset labels contain ambiguous and apparently incorrect examples; our error audit examined the highest-loss subset, not every error.
  • The out-of-distribution threshold has not been validated on a large, diverse benchmark.
  • Disposal rules vary by municipality; the impact figures in the application are illustrative assumptions, not measured outcomes.
  • The unified corpus has been built and verified but not yet trained to completion. Every Phase 2 statement in this paper concerns data, code, or method. None concerns performance.

8. Conclusion

We were given 2,527 photographs and asked to build something that works on a phone. The honest finding of this project is that the dataset, not the model, was the binding constraint. Five tuning experiments on TrashNet moved validation accuracy by less than two points and left the worst class untouched; expanding the corpus raised that class from 137 examples to 1,033 in a single step.

This is not an argument that technique does not matter. The two-phase fine-tuning schedule, the pre-declared acceptance gate, the per-class recall requirement, and the parameter-capped backbone selection all shaped the result. It is an argument about ordering. When a model plateaus and successive clever ideas trade errors instead of removing them, the next thing to examine is what the model has been allowed to see — and for a classifier meant to work on a photograph taken at a bin, 2,527 studio images against a white backdrop was never going to be enough.
